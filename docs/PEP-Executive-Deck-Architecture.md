## SLIDE 7 — Why This Channel Is Different

**1. Slide title**
Utility-Based Reach vs. Interruptive Media

**2. Executive headline**
A billboard is seen and a radio spot is heard, but a SchoolBridge notification is acted upon — because it is wired into the things a parent cannot ignore.

**3. Core message**
Utility-based reach — being present inside a tool people use because they need it — is the rarest and most defensible form of reach in the attention economy, and the school channel is one of the last large-scale examples no retailer has claimed.

**4. Supporting points**
- Interruptive media is getting weaker and more expensive: ad-blocking, scroll-past, banner-blindness, rising acquisition costs.
- The school message arrives inside an attended, intentional task — attendance alerts, reports, fee payments — not against the user's attention.
- It inherits institutional trust; interruptive media is recognised as advertising and discounted.
- Targeting is native (province/district/school/grade/role), not proxy-based; frequency recurs naturally through the calendar.
- Measurement is direct (redemptions, clicks, funnels) and channel ownership is exclusive once claimed.

**6. Charts or graphics**
**Seven-row comparison matrix** (the brief's §3.3 table). Rows: Attention · Trust · Targeting · Frequency · Measurement · Cost trajectory · Defensibility. Two columns. Use a subtle red highlight down the SchoolBridge column to signal directional advantage. **7. Speaker notes**

One trusted household relationship compounds across five Pepkor business units — making this a group asset, not a line item.
