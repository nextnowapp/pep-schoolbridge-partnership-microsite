## 1. Executive Summary

> **The thesis in one line:** PEP / Pepkor has a narrow, time-boxed opportunity to become the first major retail brand to *own* the South African school ecosystem as a trusted, always-on digital channel into millions of households — before a bank, a mobile operator, or a rival value retailer claims that ground ahead of Pepkor's April 2027 bank launch.

### 1.1 The strategic situation

Pepkor is no longer a clothing-and-value-retail business that happens to sell a few financial products. According to its own published reporting, Pepkor is deliberately expanding beyond clothing and value retail into fintech, mobile connectivity, informal-market services and financial services, with the fintech segment positioned as a core growth engine of the group. The group reported half-year earnings up 10.3% (Reuters, 26 May 2026), and has stated its intention to **launch a bank in April 2027, targeting 1.8 million primary banking customers within five years.**

That single fact — *a bank, in April 2027, chasing 1.8 million primary-banking relationships* — reframes everything. A primary-banking relationship is not won on the day an account is opened. It is won in the months and years beforehand, through repeated, low-friction, trust-building contact with a household. The brands that will win primary-banking share in 2027–2032 are the brands that already live inside the daily routines of South African families in 2026.

There is no daily routine more universal, more emotionally weighted, or more under-monetised than **school**.

### 1.2 The asset

The **SchoolBridge Ecosystem** is a connected family of platforms — already built, already live, already operating inside real schools and a real provincial structure. It is the digital operating system of the school: administration, parent communication, attendance, academics, reporting, admissions, fee collection and payments, and learner/staff mobile apps. It reaches the household not as an advertiser interrupting attention, but as a *utility the household already depends on* — the channel through which a parent learns their child was absent, pays a school fee, receives a report card, or completes an enrolment.

This is the difference that matters. A billboard is seen. A radio spot is heard. A SchoolBridge notification is *acted upon*, because it is wired into the things a parent cannot ignore. Utility-based reach is the rarest and most defensible form of reach in the modern attention economy, and the school channel is one of the last large-scale examples of it that no major retail brand in South Africa has yet claimed.

> **Proof of execution, not concept.** This is not a pitch for something to be built. Live partner microsites already exist — including **pep.provincebridge.co.za** (a PEP-branded provincial environment) and the ecosystem pricing site **pricebook.schoolbridge.co.za**. ProvinceBridge already demonstrates the architecture for monitoring schools, campaigns, attendance, admissions and performance at provincial scale. The question on the table is not *can this work* — it is *who owns it*.

### 1.3 The commercial logic, in headline form

Three forces converge to make this opportunity unusually time-sensitive for PEP specifically:

1. **The household-trust clock.** Pepkor's bank launches in under a year (April 2027). The bank's five-year target of 1.8 million primary customers will be far cheaper to hit if Pepkor has already built warm, branded, trusted contact with school households *before* launch — rather than acquiring those relationships cold, through paid media, after a competitor bank has done the same.

2. **The channel-ownership clock.** The school ecosystem is a *category that does not yet have an owner*. Category ownership is a one-time prize. The first major brand to embed itself as "the brand that powers your school's digital life" captures a positioning that the second, third and fourth movers cannot buy back at any price. Whoever moves first sets the terms; everyone else competes for leftovers or pays a premium to displace an incumbent.

3. **The annual spend clock.** Back-to-school is one of the largest concentrated household-spend moments in the South African retail calendar — uniforms, stationery, shoes, devices, connectivity. PEP already wins a meaningful share of this spend in-store. The school channel lets PEP reach the *exact household, at the exact decision moment, through the exact institution* that triggers that spend — and convert it with digital vouchers, store-locator routing, and PAXI fulfilment.

Crucially, the value compounds *across the Pepkor group*, not within a single business unit. The same channel that drives a back-to-school apparel campaign also drives **PEP Cell** SIM, prepaid, airtime, data and device sales; carries **bill-payment and cash-in/cash-out** messaging via **Flash**; routes parcels and click-and-collect through **PAXI**; and — when the time comes — warms households for the **bank**. One channel, one relationship, many monetisation surfaces.

### 1.4 What PEP gets — and what it forfeits by waiting

If PEP acts, it acquires a *proprietary, trusted, daily channel into the South African schooling household* — segmentable by province, district, school, grade and role (parent / learner / teacher) — at a cost structure dramatically below paid media, and with measurement that paid media struggles to match (voucher redemptions, store-locator clicks, conversion funnels, district-level performance).

If PEP waits, the most likely outcome is not that the opportunity persists unchanged. It is that a competitor — another value retailer, a mobile network operator hunting prepaid share, or a rival bank chasing the same primary-banking households Pepkor will chase in 2027 — embeds *first*, and PEP is left buying interruptive media against an incumbent who owns the trusted channel. The asymmetry is stark: the cost of moving first is a bounded pilot; the cost of moving second is a permanent structural disadvantage in the exact customer segment Pepkor's bank strategy depends on.

### 1.5 The recommended next step

We do not recommend a group-wide commitment today. We recommend a **90-day pilot across 50–100 schools**, anchored to a single, measurable commercial objective — either a **back-to-school retail campaign** or a **PEP Cell connectivity campaign** — with engagement and retail-conversion tracking from day one, clear success criteria agreed up front, and a renewal decision gate at day 90.

The pilot is designed to answer one question with real data: *does the school channel convert?* If it does, the roadmap scales it from pilot to district to province to group-wide ecosystem, deliberately timed so that household trust is at scale *before* the April 2027 bank launch. If it does not, PEP has spent a contained, defined amount and learned something valuable about its customer base.

> **Bottom line for the CEO:** This is a low-risk, high-asymmetry move to own a new channel category that maps directly onto Pepkor's stated fintech-and-banking growth strategy, timed against a clock that is already running. The downside is a bounded pilot. The upside is becoming the brand that South African families associate with their children's school life — and carrying that trust straight into the bank launch. The recommendation is to authorise the pilot.

## 2. Context & Market Opportunity

### 2.1 The South African schooling household as a commercial environment

To understand why the school channel is valuable, start with what a school actually *is* in commercial terms. A school is not a building. It is a **persistent, high-frequency, high-trust relationship hub** connecting a stable, geographically-clustered population of households around a shared, emotionally-central concern: a child's education and wellbeing.

Consider the properties of this environment from a marketer's or a financial-services strategist's point of view:

- **Universality.** Schooling is close to universal across income bands. The school household is not a niche — it spans the full breadth of the South African consumer market, from formal-sector salaried families to the informal and township economy.
- **Stability and frequency.** Unlike most consumer touchpoints, the school relationship recurs daily during term and persists for years per child — and often a decade or more per household with multiple children. Few commercial relationships enjoy that kind of durability and cadence.
- **Trust by association.** Communication that arrives *through the school* inherits a portion of the school's institutional trust. A message about attendance, fees, or a report card is opened and acted upon because it is not perceived as advertising — it is perceived as part of being a responsible parent.
- **Natural segmentation.** A school self-segments its population by geography, by grade (and therefore child age), by role (parent, learner, teacher), and increasingly by digital engagement. This is exactly the segmentation a retailer or a bank would otherwise pay dearly to construct.
- **Emotional weight.** Spend connected to a child's schooling is among the most defended in the household budget. Parents who will trade down on their own clothing will protect spend on a child's uniform, shoes, stationery and connectivity.

> **The core insight:** The school is one of the highest-trust, highest-frequency, most naturally-segmented touchpoints into the South African household that exists — and it is almost entirely un-owned as a commercial channel. That combination is rare. Trust plus frequency plus segmentation plus un-owned is, in channel terms, a greenfield.

### 2.2 The back-to-school and school-year spend cycle

The South African retail calendar has a small number of genuine peak demand moments. Back-to-school — broadly the December-through-late-January window when families purchase for the new academic year — is widely recognised as one of the largest concentrated household-spend events of the year *(this seasonality is broadly known South African retail context; the specific share-of-wallet and rand figures below are labelled illustrative).* The basket is unusually broad and maps directly onto PEP's core ranges and the wider group:

| Back-to-school need | Maps to PEP / Pepkor capability |
|---|---|
| School uniforms, shoes, sportswear | PEP core value apparel |
| Stationery, bags, lunchboxes | PEP general merchandise |
| Mobile phone / device for a learner | PEP Cell devices |
| SIM, prepaid airtime, data bundle | PEP Cell connectivity |
| School-fee payment / instalments | PayBridge + Flash bill payments / cash-in |
| Getting goods to the household | PAXI parcel & click-and-collect |

The strategic point is not merely that PEP sells these things. It is that **the school is the institution that *triggers* this entire basket**, and the school channel reaches the household at the precise moment the trigger fires — when the school communicates dates, requirements, fee schedules and stationery lists. A brand present *inside that communication flow* reaches demand at the point of formation, not after it has dispersed across competitors.

Beyond the December–January peak, the school year generates a rolling calendar of secondary spend moments: each term's reopening, sports seasons (boots, kit), examination periods (study aids, devices, data), winter-uniform switches, and end-of-year events. A channel embedded in the school calendar can synchronise commercial messaging to these moments with a precision that broadcast media cannot approach.

> **Illustrative sizing (clearly labelled as estimate).** *To frame — not to assert — scale:* if one assumes (illustratively) a large public-school learner population in the low tens of millions and a meaningful back-to-school basket per household, the aggregate annual back-to-school spend across the categories above plausibly runs into the tens of billions of rand. **This figure is an illustrative estimate / assumption used only to frame addressable scale; it is not a sourced number and must not be presented as fact.** The defensible point that does not depend on the estimate: back-to-school is a top-tier annual retail event, PEP already competes hard for it, and the school channel reaches its exact trigger point.

### 2.3 The under-monetised, trusted nature of the school channel

Most high-trust institutional channels in a consumer's life have already been commercialised — banking apps carry offers, retailers run loyalty programmes, telcos push bundles. The school channel is a conspicuous exception. Schools communicate with parents constantly, but that communication is almost never a *managed commercial channel*. The trust is high and the monetisation is near zero. Economically, that is an arbitrage.

The reason it has stayed un-monetised is not lack of value — it is lack of *infrastructure*. There has historically been no clean, consented, brand-safe, segmentable way for a national brand to reach school households *through* the school without compromising the school's trust or creating administrative burden. SchoolBridge is precisely that infrastructure: it gives the school a genuine operating utility (the reason parents engage) and, alongside it, a governed, consented surface through which a sponsoring brand can reach those households appropriately. The platform creates the channel; the channel is what PEP would own.

### 2.4 PEP's customer-base overlap with school households

PEP's brand proposition — value, accessibility, ubiquity, trusted affordability — is built for exactly the demographic that dominates the school-household population: price-conscious, family-centred South African consumers across formal and informal economies. *(The strength and breadth of PEP's value-retail positioning is drawn from Pepkor's published "Enabling affordable living" and integrated reporting; the precise overlap percentage below is illustrative.)*

> **Illustrative estimate.** It is reasonable to assume a very high overlap — *(illustratively, a substantial majority)* — between PEP's existing customer base and households with school-going children, given PEP's national footprint and value positioning. **This overlap figure is an illustrative assumption, not a measured statistic.** The strategic implication holds regardless of the exact number: PEP is not being asked to reach a *new* audience. It is being asked to reach *its own existing customers* through a *new, more trusted, more measurable channel* — and to do so at the moments those customers are making category-relevant decisions.

This reframes the investment. The school channel is not an audience-acquisition play that hopes new people discover PEP. It is a **relationship-deepening and conversion play** on the audience PEP already serves — turning ambient brand affinity into measurable, attributable action (a voucher redeemed, a store visit, a SIM activated, a fee paid through a PEP-enabled rail).

It is worth dwelling on *why this overlap is strategically richer than a simple audience match*. PEP's relationship with the value household is broad but, in channel terms, shallow and episodic — a customer walks into a store, transacts, and leaves, and PEP has limited structured, consented, repeated digital contact with that same household between visits. The school channel converts an *episodic, in-store, anonymous* relationship into a *continuous, digital, identified-at-the-household-level* one. That is a qualitative upgrade in the relationship, not merely additional reach. For a group whose published strategy is pivoting toward financial services — where continuous, identified, trusted relationships are the entire basis of the business model — that upgrade is precisely the capability the strategy requires.

### 2.5 The parent purchase-decision journey

The school channel is unusually well-positioned within the parent's actual decision journey for school-related spend. That journey, in simplified form, runs: *(1) the school signals a requirement* (a stationery list, a uniform standard, a fee schedule, a device or data need for online learning) → *(2) the parent forms an intention to buy* → *(3) the parent chooses where to buy* → *(4) the parent transacts* → *(5) goods reach the household.*

Conventional retail media can only reliably reach the parent at stages 3–4, and only by buying broad attention and hoping to be present when intent happens to form. The school channel is structurally present at **stage 1 — the trigger itself** — because the requirement is communicated *through the platform*. A brand present at the trigger shapes the intention before competitors are even aware it has formed. It then carries the parent through every subsequent stage on a single rail: requirement (in-channel) → offer and voucher (in-channel) → where-to-buy (store-locator) → transaction (in-store / PEP-enabled payment) → fulfilment (PAXI). No competing channel owns that full journey from trigger to doorstep.

> **The journey insight:** Owning stage 1 (the trigger) is worth more than owning stages 3–4 (the purchase), because intent captured at formation is cheaper to convert and harder for a competitor to contest than intent contested at the shelf. The school channel is one of the only ways for a retailer to be present at the trigger of an entire annual spend category.

### 2.6 The teacher as a high-trust influence node

A dimension that is easy to overlook: the channel reaches **teachers** through StaffBridge, and teachers are not merely additional consumers — they are among the most-trusted figures in their communities. A teacher's casual endorsement, or simply a teacher's evident familiarity with a brand inside the school's own platform, carries a halo into the households that teacher serves. In township and rural communities especially, the teacher is frequently a more credible voice on matters of money, devices and services than any advertisement. Reaching teachers well, in a professional and respectful context (their working app), is therefore a force-multiplier on the channel's household reach — not a separate, lesser audience. We do not over-claim a measurable mechanism here; the point is qualitative but real, and PEP can test it as part of the pilot.

### 2.7 The informal and township-economy opportunity

Pepkor's published strategy explicitly leans into the informal market — Flash's informal-market fintech platform, cash-in/cash-out, bill payments and prepaid services are designed for exactly the township and informal economy where formal digital channels are thinnest. *(The informal-market focus of Flash and Pepkor's services strategy is from Pepkor's published reporting; the scale characterisation of the township economy below is broadly-known context.)*

Schools in township and peri-urban communities are often the single most reliable, most-trusted institutional node in the community — frequently more present in daily life than a bank branch or a formal retailer. This makes the school channel disproportionately powerful precisely where Pepkor's fintech and future-bank ambitions need the most help:

- It reaches households that paid digital media reaches poorly and expensively.
- It carries the trust of a respected local institution into communities that are, justifiably, cautious about unfamiliar financial offers.
- It can route a household from a school notification, to a nearby PEP store (store-locator), to an in-store cash or Flash transaction, to a PAXI collection — a fully-bridged physical-digital journey in a community where that journey is otherwise fragmented.

> **The informal-economy thesis:** In the formal market, the school channel is *valuable*. In the informal and township economy, it is potentially *decisive* — because it is one of the few high-trust digital bridges into households that Pepkor's fintech and banking strategy must reach, and that competitors will find hard and expensive to reach by any other means.

### 2.8 Sizing the addressable opportunity (framed, not asserted)

We deliberately avoid a single hero number. Instead, we frame the opportunity as the product of three things PEP can size against its own internal data:

1. **Reach** — the number of school households the ecosystem can connect (scalable from pilot to provincial to national).
2. **Frequency** — the cadence of trusted, consented contact per household per term.
3. **Conversion surface** — the breadth of monetisation: apparel, general merchandise, PEP Cell connectivity and devices, Flash/bill-payment services, PAXI fulfilment, and future-bank acquisition.

> **Addressable-opportunity framing.** The commercial value to PEP = (school households reached) × (relevant, trusted contacts per household) × (conversion across the full group surface) − (channel cost, which is materially below paid media). Each input scales with the roadmap in Section 13. We recommend PEP populate this frame with its own first-party economics during the pilot rather than rely on any external estimate. *Every specific number anywhere in this brief that is not tied to a cited source is an illustrative assumption, consolidated in the Appendix.*

## 3. The SchoolBridge Ecosystem as a Channel

### 3.1 What the platform is

SchoolBridge is not a single app. It is a connected ecosystem in which each component owns a part of school life, and together they form the digital operating system of the school — and, by extension, a governed channel into the household.

| Platform | Role in the ecosystem | Who it touches |
|---|---|---|
| **SchoolBridge** | The digital operating system for an individual school: administration, communication, attendance, academics, reporting, school website, stakeholder management. | School leadership, admin staff, teachers, parents |
| **ProvinceBridge** | Provincial / district command centre: monitor schools, campaigns, attendance, admissions and performance at scale. | Departments, districts, provincial leadership |
| **AdmissionBridge** | Year-round digital admissions and enrolment intelligence. | Prospective parents, school admins |
| **StudyBridge** | Learning outcomes and intervention. | Learners, teachers, parents |
| **PayBridge** | School-fee collection and payment automation (Sage, Netcash integrations). | Parents, school finance offices |
| **StudentBridge** | Learner mobile app. | Learners (incl. teens 15–19) |
| **StaffBridge** | Staff mobile app. | Teachers and school staff |

The significance of this architecture is that it touches *every constituency in the school household relationship* — the parent who pays and receives communication, the learner who is the future consumer, the teacher who is both an influential community figure and a consumer in their own right, and the district/provincial layer that provides scale and credibility.

### 3.2 How it reaches parents, learners and teachers

The channel reaches people because they have a *reason* to be there that has nothing to do with advertising:

- **Parents** open SchoolBridge to read attendance alerts, receive school communication, check a report, complete an admission, or pay a fee through PayBridge. These are non-skippable life-admin tasks. A PEP presence adjacent to these tasks is seen in a context of attention and trust, not interruption.
- **Learners** — particularly teens 15–19 using StudentBridge — engage with their own academic and school-life content. This is a notoriously hard-to-reach, ad-averse, brand-forming demographic, reached here in a native, non-interruptive context.
- **Teachers** use StaffBridge for their working day. Teachers are high-trust community influencers; reaching them well has a halo effect into the households they serve.

Each of these is a *consented, contextual, role-appropriate* surface — not a scraped list and not a bought audience. That distinction is the entire value proposition.

### 3.3 Why utility-based reach beats interruptive media

The modern attention economy has made interruptive media progressively weaker and more expensive: ad-blocking, scroll-past behaviour, banner-blindness, skippable video, and rising acquisition costs. Against that backdrop, *utility-based reach* — being present inside a tool people use because they need it — is structurally advantaged on every dimension that matters.

| Dimension | Interruptive media (billboard, radio, social ads) | Utility-based reach (SchoolBridge channel) |
|---|---|---|
| Attention | Competes against, and is avoided by, the user | Arrives inside an attended, intentional task |
| Trust | Recognised as advertising; discounted accordingly | Inherits institutional (school) trust |
| Targeting | Broad or proxy-based | Native: province / district / school / grade / role |
| Frequency | Costly to sustain | Naturally recurring through the school calendar |
| Measurement | Largely inferred | Direct: redemptions, clicks, funnels, district performance |
| Cost trajectory | Rising | Materially below paid media; improves with scale |
| Defensibility | Anyone can buy the same media | Channel ownership is exclusive once claimed |

> **Key message:** Interruptive media rents attention by the impression and competes with the very person it targets. The school channel earns attention because it is wired into things a household cannot ignore. PEP would not be buying impressions — it would be owning a relationship.

### 3.4 Proof of execution

This is a built, live ecosystem, and the proof is concrete:

- **pep.provincebridge.co.za** — a PEP-branded provincial environment already stood up within the ecosystem, demonstrating that the channel can carry the PEP brand at provincial scale today, not someday.
- **pricebook.schoolbridge.co.za** — the live ecosystem pricing site, demonstrating commercial maturity and a productised, priced offering rather than a concept.
- **ProvinceBridge** as a working command-centre architecture — the proof that the ecosystem already operates at the district/provincial layer that gives a national brand campaign its scale, governance and reporting.

> **De-risking the partnership:** Because the platform is built and live, the partnership question is reduced from "can we build a channel?" to "how fast do we scale a channel that already works?" That is a fundamentally different — and far lower — risk profile than a typical innovation or sponsorship bet.

## 4. Competitive Landscape & First-Mover Advantage

### 4.1 A new, uncontested channel *category*

The most important fact about the competitive landscape is that, today, there is no incumbent. The "sponsored digital school ecosystem" is not a contested market with established leaders — it is a *category that does not yet have an owner*. Categories without owners are the rarest and most valuable situation in marketing strategy, because the first credible mover does not win share — it *defines the category and installs itself as the default*.

This is categorically different from PEP buying more of an existing media type. When PEP buys radio, it competes against every other radio buyer at market price. When PEP claims the school channel, it competes against *no one* — until someone else moves. The window in which a category has no owner is, by definition, temporary.

### 4.2 Who could move into this space

We name the categories of potential competitors candidly — without overclaiming that any specific competitor is currently acting — because PEP should weigh the *risk of being second*, not a hypothetical certainty.

| Competitor category | Why they might want the school channel | What they would bring | What PEP would lose if they move first |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Other value retailers** | Same back-to-school basket, same value-household audience | Comparable scale and store footprint | Direct loss of PEP's most natural channel to a direct rival |
| **Mobile network operators (MNOs)** | Hunting prepaid/data and youth (StudentBridge) share | Deep telco budgets, device subsidies | PEP Cell's most efficient growth channel captured by a pure-play telco |
| **Banks (incl. new entrants)** | Early acquisition of household/primary-banking relationships | Large acquisition budgets, financial products | The exact pre-bank household-trust advantage Pepkor needs for April 2027 |
| **FMCG brands** | Reach into family households at scale | Big brand budgets | Channel cluttered or fragmented; PEP loses clean ownership |
| **Student-discount / youth platforms** | Own the teen/learner audience | Youth-native mechanics | PEP loses native access to the 15–19 future-customer segment |
| **School-payment / edtech apps** | Already adjacent to the fee/payment flow | Existing school relationships | A payments rival embeds before PayBridge-PEP synergy is captured |

The two categories PEP should weigh most heavily are **MNOs** (because they directly threaten PEP Cell's growth) and **banks / new bank entrants** (because they directly threaten the pre-launch household-trust advantage that makes Pepkor's own bank cheaper to build).

### 4.3 What PEP forfeits if a competitor moves first

First-mover disadvantage in channel ownership is not symmetric with first-mover advantage — it is worse. If a competitor embeds first:

1. **The trust position is taken.** Households will associate "the brand that powers our school's digital life" with whoever got there first. Trust positions are sticky; displacing an incumbent is expensive and often impossible.
2. **PEP is pushed back into interruptive media** — buying impressions against a competitor who owns the trusted channel, at a structural cost and credibility disadvantage.
3. **PEP Cell's most efficient growth path closes** if an MNO claims the channel.
4. **The bank launch loses its pre-warmed audience** if a rival bank embeds first — meaning Pepkor pays cold-acquisition prices for the 1.8 million primary customers it could have warmed at a fraction of the cost.

> **The asymmetry, stated plainly:** Moving first costs a bounded pilot. Moving second costs a permanent structural disadvantage in the precise customer segment Pepkor's group strategy depends on. The downside of acting is small and contained; the downside of *not* acting compounds and is hard to reverse.

### 4.4 Category-ownership economics

Owning a category — rather than buying into one — changes the economics in PEP's favour in three durable ways:

- **Pricing power on the channel.** An owner sets terms and can negotiate exclusivity; a buyer pays the going rate. (See exclusivity considerations in Section 9.)
- **Compounding trust.** Each trusted, useful interaction makes the next one more effective. Interruptive media does not compound — every impression starts cold. The school channel improves with tenure.
- **Defensive moat.** Exclusivity plus an installed trust position is a moat. It does not merely help PEP; it *denies* the channel to competitors, which protects PEP Cell and the future bank simultaneously.

> **Key message:** This is a one-time category-ownership prize with a defensive dimension. PEP is not only buying growth — it is denying a strategic channel to the MNOs and banks it will compete with hardest in 2027 and beyond.

### 4.5 The "build vs. buy vs. partner" question

A natural executive challenge is: *if the school channel is so valuable, why not build it ourselves?* The answer is that channel ownership of this kind is won on two things PEP cannot simply purchase — *institutional trust inside schools* and *a live, integrated platform that schools actually run their day on*. Both take years to earn and are fragile to manufacture from a retail brand's standing start, because schools are rightly wary of a retailer arriving to "build a platform" whose primary purpose is to sell to their families.

The three options compare as follows:

| Option | Time to channel | Trust dynamics | Risk | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Build** (PEP creates its own school platform) | Years; must earn school adoption from zero | A retailer-built school tool reads as self-interested; adoption is hard | High execution + reputational risk; competitors move while PEP builds | Slow and risky |
| **Buy** (acquire a school platform) | Faster, but integration and trust still take time | Acquisition can taint a trusted neutral platform if mishandled | Capital-heavy; integration risk | Possible later, not the entry move |
| **Partner** (sponsor the live SchoolBridge ecosystem) | Immediate — the platform is already live and trusted | The platform's neutrality and utility are preserved; PEP rides existing trust | Bounded to a pilot; reversible | **Recommended entry** |

Partnering captures the speed of buying and the low risk of a pilot, while preserving the very trust-neutrality that makes the channel work — trust a retailer-built tool would struggle to earn. The platform already exists, is live, and carries the PEP brand at provincial scale today (pep.provincebridge.co.za). The fastest, lowest-risk route to channel ownership is therefore to partner now and retain the option to deepen — not to spend years building a rival the schools may never trust.

### 4.6 Why a fast follower cannot simply copy PEP

Suppose PEP moves first and a competitor tries to follow. What stops a well-funded rival from replicating the move six months later? Three structural barriers:

1. **Exclusivity (contractual).** A category-exclusive headline sponsorship simply closes the door to the value-retail category. A follower cannot buy what is no longer for sale.
2. **Installed trust (behavioural).** Once households associate the school's digital life with PEP, a follower must *displace* an incumbent, which is far costlier than arriving to an open position. Trust positions, once set, are sticky.
3. **Compounding data and integration (operational).** Each campaign PEP runs deepens its understanding of the school household and tightens its cross-BU integration (PEP Cell, Flash, PAXI, bank). A follower starts that learning curve from zero while PEP is already several cycles ahead.

> **The follower's problem:** A fast follower does not face an empty field — it faces a contractually-closed category, an installed trust position, and an opponent several conversion cycles up the learning curve. That is exactly the defensible position PEP would be buying, and exactly the position PEP would be conceding if it lets someone else move first.

## 5. Why Now

### 5.1 Pepkor's strategic pivot is the context

Pepkor's published direction is unambiguous: expansion beyond clothing and value retail into fintech, mobile connectivity, informal-market services and financial services, with fintech positioned as a core growth engine and half-year earnings up 10.3% (Reuters, 26 May 2026). Flash, bill payments, cash-in/cash-out, PEP Cell mobile and the planned bank are not side bets — they are the growth thesis.

A growth thesis built on *household financial relationships* needs *household trust and household reach at scale*. The school channel is one of the most efficient ways to manufacture exactly that. The pivot and the channel are not coincidentally aligned; they are the same strategy viewed from two angles.

### 5.2 The April 2027 bank launch is the strategic clock

The single most important date in this brief is **April 2027**, when Pepkor has stated it will launch a bank, targeting **1.8 million primary banking customers within five years.**

Two facts about banking make this date a hard deadline for the school-channel decision:

1. **Primary-banking relationships are won before account opening.** People choose a primary bank through accumulated trust and familiarity, not a single ad. The cheapest 1.8 million primary customers are the ones who already trust the Pepkor brand inside their daily lives *before* the bank opens.
2. **Trust takes time to build at scale.** Embedding into school households, building cadence, and earning the "this brand is part of our school life" association is a months-to-quarters process — not a launch-week campaign.

> **Do the arithmetic of the clock.** It is June 2026. The bank launches April 2027 — roughly ten months out. To have household trust *at scale before launch*, the channel must be piloted, proven and scaling *now*. A pilot started this quarter can reach proven, scaling status well inside the pre-launch window. A decision deferred six months collapses that runway and forces the bank to acquire its primary customers cold, at market cost, possibly against a rival who used the channel PEP declined.

### 5.3 Back-to-school timing reinforces the clock

The next major back-to-school cycle (the December 2026–January 2027 window) is the ideal proving ground and the ideal warm-up for the bank: it is the highest-intent retail moment, it falls *immediately before* the April 2027 bank launch, and it lets PEP demonstrate hard retail conversion (vouchers, store visits, PEP Cell activations) while simultaneously building the household familiarity the bank will inherit. Missing that cycle means waiting a full year for the next one — and launching the bank without a back-to-school warm-up.

### 5.4 Digital-adoption tailwinds

South Africa's high mobile and prepaid penetration, and the steady migration of school administration and parent communication onto digital platforms, mean the channel's reach is expanding on its own — the addressable base of digitally-reachable school households grows every year. *(High mobile/prepaid penetration is broadly-known South African context; specific penetration percentages are not asserted here.)* Moving now means PEP rides an adoption curve that is still climbing, rather than buying into a saturated one later.

> **Why now, in one line:** The pivot makes the channel *strategically relevant*; the April 2027 bank launch makes it *urgent*; the upcoming back-to-school cycle makes it *immediately actionable*; and digital-adoption tailwinds make it *cheaper to enter today than it will ever be again*.

## 6. Strategic Fit

The school channel is not a fit for one PEP business unit. It is a fit for the *group*, because the same trusted household relationship can be monetised across every Pepkor capability. This section maps each asset to a SchoolBridge use case and shows how the value compounds.

### 6.1 Asset-by-asset mapping

| Pepkor / PEP asset | What it is (per published sources) | SchoolBridge use case | How the channel helps |
|---|---|---|---|
| **PEP value retail** | Core value clothing & general merchandise; "enabling affordable living" | Back-to-school & school-calendar campaigns; parent household campaigns | Reaches the school-triggered basket at the moment of formation, with vouchers + store-locator |
| **PEP Cell** | Mobile/cellular: phones, SIM, prepaid, airtime, data | Learner & parent connectivity campaigns; device upgrades; data for study | Native access to teens (StudentBridge) and parents; ties device/SIM/data to school need |
| **PAXI** | Parcel/logistics leveraging the store footprint as a services platform | Fulfilment for school-driven purchases; click-and-collect | Bridges digital school notification → physical collection at a PEP store |
| **Flash** | Major informal-market fintech platform | School-fee payments (with PayBridge), bill payments, cash-in/out | Routes the school payment flow through Pepkor rails, especially in the informal economy |
| **Bill payments & cash-in/cash-out** | High-volume cash & bill-payment processing | Fee reminders → pay in-store; airtime/data top-up prompts | Converts school admin moments into store-traffic and transaction volume |
| **Future bank (April 2027)** | Bank targeting 1.8m primary customers in 5 years | Pre-launch household trust; launch readiness; account acquisition | Warm, trusted, segmented household base ready at launch instead of cold acquisition |

### 6.2 How the channel compounds across the group

The strategic power is in the *compounding*, not in any single mapping. A single school household, reached once through SchoolBridge, can be:

1. Sold a back-to-school apparel basket (PEP retail),
2. Activated on a SIM and data bundle for the learner (PEP Cell),
3. Fulfilled via parcel collection at a PEP store (PAXI),
4. Converted into a recurring fee-payment and bill-payment relationship (PayBridge + Flash),
5. And then warmed into a primary-banking relationship at launch (the bank).

Each step deepens the relationship and lowers the cost of the next, because the trust and data accrued at each stage carry forward. This is the opposite of interruptive media, where every campaign re-acquires attention from scratch. The school channel is a *relationship compounding machine* — and the more Pepkor business units plug into it, the more efficient each one becomes.

> **The group-level insight:** Most channels serve one business unit. This channel serves retail, connectivity, logistics, fintech and the bank — from a *single* household relationship. That is why the decision belongs at group level, not in any one BU's budget: the channel's value is precisely the cross-BU compounding that no single BU would capture or fund on its own.

### 6.2a A worked household journey (illustrative)

To make the compounding concrete, consider a single illustrative household — the Mokoenas, a value-conscious family with two school-going children. *(This is an illustrative composite, not a real customer; it shows the mechanism, not a guaranteed outcome.)*

1. **January (back-to-school).** The school posts the new-year requirements through SchoolBridge. Adjacent to that notification, the family receives a PEP back-to-school voucher. They redeem it in-store for uniforms, shoes and stationery. *(PEP retail revenue captured at the trigger.)*
2. **February (connectivity).** The elder child needs data for StudyBridge coursework. A PEP Cell offer arrives in context; the family buys a SIM and a data bundle. *(PEP Cell activation; a recurring top-up relationship begins.)*
3. **March (fee payment).** The term fee falls due. Through PayBridge, the family pays — and the payment is routed via Pepkor rails (Flash / bill payments), with the option to pay in-store as a cash-in transaction. *(Fintech transaction volume; a recurring payment relationship.)*
4. **Ongoing (fulfilment).** A mid-term purchase is fulfilled via PAXI click-and-collect at the nearest PEP store. *(PAXI volume; another store visit.)*
5. **2027 (the bank).** When Pepkor's bank launches in April 2027, the Mokoenas have spent fifteen months experiencing PEP as a trusted, useful presence in their school life. They are a *warm* prospect — far cheaper to convert to a primary-banking relationship than a household acquired cold through paid media. *(Primary-banking acquisition at a fraction of cold cost.)*

Each step was triggered by a school moment, reached through a single trusted channel, and monetised by a different Pepkor business unit — and the whole sequence pre-positioned the family for the bank. That is the compounding machine in one household. Multiply it across a province, and it is the engine of the bank's 1.8-million-customer target.

### 6.2b The bank advantage, examined

Because the April 2027 bank is the strategic centre of gravity, it deserves a closer look at *why* the school channel matters specifically to it.

A bank's hardest and most expensive task is winning *primary* relationships — the account a customer treats as their financial home — as opposed to secondary or dormant accounts. Primary status is won on trust, habit and low-friction familiarity, accumulated over time. A new bank with no branch heritage faces this from a standing start and typically pays heavily in acquisition marketing to overcome it.

The school channel directly attacks that cost in three ways:

- **Pre-warming.** Households that have experienced PEP as a trusted, useful presence for months before launch require far less persuasion at launch. Trust is pre-paid.
- **Segmentation for targeting.** The channel knows households by province, district, school and role — letting the bank concentrate launch effort where conversion is likeliest, rather than spraying national media.
- **A built-in habit loop.** Households already paying fees and topping up connectivity through Pepkor-adjacent rails are a short, natural step from banking with Pepkor.

> **The bank-readiness thesis:** The school channel is, in effect, a *pre-launch primary-banking acquisition engine* disguised as a retail-and-connectivity channel. Every household it warms before April 2027 is a household the bank does not have to acquire cold. Against a five-year target of 1.8 million primary customers, even a modest reduction in cost-per-acquisition across a warmed base is a material strategic and financial prize.

### 6.3 Fit with the school's interests (the alignment that makes it durable)

A partnership that benefits only PEP is fragile. This one is durable because it is genuinely three-sided:

- **The school** gains a real operating platform (admin, communication, attendance, payments) it would otherwise have to fund itself — sponsorship can make the utility more affordable or richer for schools.
- **The household** gains useful, contextual, money-saving offers (back-to-school vouchers, affordable connectivity, easier fee payment) at exactly the moments it needs them.
- **PEP** gains the trusted, measurable channel.

> **Critical positioning:** SchoolBridge *complements* — and does not replace — any government, provincial or departmental system (e.g. GDE platforms). It is an operating layer that works alongside official systems, never a substitute for them. The partnership is a *strategic commercial alliance* in which all three parties gain — explicitly **not** a charity hand-out and **not** a displacement of public infrastructure. That alignment is what makes the channel defensible and durable rather than politically or reputationally fragile.

## 7. Commercial Use Cases

Each use case below is specified with **audience, mechanic, conversion pathway, and a success metric**, so the commercial logic is concrete rather than aspirational.

### 7.1 Back-to-School campaigns

- **Audience:** Parents of school-going children, segmented by grade (and therefore basket), province, district and school.
- **Mechanic:** School-calendar-synchronised messaging through SchoolBridge in the December–January window — stationery lists, uniform reminders, fee-schedule notices — carrying PEP back-to-school offers and digital vouchers.
- **Conversion pathway:** Notification → digital voucher → store-locator click → in-store redemption (and/or PAXI click-and-collect).
- **Success metric:** Voucher redemption rate and incremental back-to-school basket value per reached household vs. a control group.

### 7.2 Teen retail conversion (15–19)

- **Audience:** Learners aged 15–19 on StudentBridge — the hardest-to-reach, most brand-forming, most ad-averse segment.
- **Mechanic:** Native, non-interruptive placements and youth-relevant offers (fashion, devices, prepaid, data) inside the learner's own school-life app context.
- **Conversion pathway:** In-app offer → device/SIM/data or apparel purchase → loyalty into early adulthood (and a head start on the future bank's youth segment).
- **Success metric:** Teen engagement and conversion rate; cohort tracking of repeat purchase as the cohort ages toward primary-banking age.

### 7.3 Parent household campaigns

- **Audience:** Parents, reached during high-trust admin tasks (attendance, reports, fee payment).
- **Mechanic:** Contextual household offers — value apparel, general merchandise, connectivity, and Flash/bill-payment prompts — adjacent to the admin task, not interrupting it.
- **Conversion pathway:** Admin task → adjacent relevant offer → store visit / transaction.
- **Success metric:** Click-through and store-visit/transaction conversion per parent reached; lift in cross-category purchase.

### 7.4 PEP Cell growth (SIM, prepaid, devices, data)

- **Audience:** Learners needing devices/data for study; parents managing family connectivity.
- **Mechanic:** Tie connectivity directly to school need — "your child needs data for StudyBridge / exam season" — with device, SIM, prepaid and data offers.
- **Conversion pathway:** School-need trigger → PEP Cell offer → SIM activation / device purchase / recurring data top-up.
- **Success metric:** SIM activations, device units, and recurring data-top-up volume attributable to the channel.

### 7.5 Future-bank launch readiness

- **Audience:** School households, pre-warmed before April 2027.
- **Mechanic:** Build trusted, branded familiarity and (where appropriate and consented) early interest registration in the months before launch, so the bank launches into a warm audience.
- **Conversion pathway:** Pre-launch trust → launch-window account acquisition → primary-banking relationship.
- **Success metric:** Pre-launch warm-audience size; launch-window account-opening conversion from school-channel households vs. cold-acquired customers (cost-per-acquisition delta).

### 7.6 Community commerce (PAXI + store + digital)

- **Audience:** Township and peri-urban households where the school is the most-trusted institutional node.
- **Mechanic:** A fully-bridged journey — school notification → nearby PEP store (locator) → in-store Flash/cash transaction or purchase → PAXI collection — designed for communities where formal digital channels are thin.
- **Conversion pathway:** Digital school touch → physical store visit → transaction → repeat.
- **Success metric:** Store-locator clicks, in-store transaction volume, and PAXI collections attributable to the channel in target communities.

> **What ties these together:** Every use case starts in a trusted school moment and ends in a measurable Pepkor transaction — and several of them feed the bank. The channel is not a brand-awareness play; it is a conversion-and-acquisition engine with attribution built in.

## 9. Sponsorship & Partnership Models

We propose three models of escalating depth. PEP can enter at any level; each is a stepping stone to the next. **All commercial structures below are explicitly proposed / illustrative — they are starting points for negotiation, not quoted prices.**

### 9.1 Model A — Headline Sponsorship

- **What it is:** PEP becomes the named headline sponsor of the SchoolBridge ecosystem (and/or specific provincial/branded environments such as the existing PEP-branded ProvinceBridge).
- **What PEP gets:** Category ownership and brand association as "the brand that powers your school's digital life"; exclusivity in the value-retail category; first call on premium placements; the defensive moat that denies the channel to competitors.
- **Indicative commercial structure (proposed / illustrative):** An annual sponsorship fee, scaled to reach (schools / households connected), with tiered pricing as the footprint grows from district to provincial to national.
- **Rights:** Brand presence across the ecosystem; co-branding of provincial environments; first-look rights on new ecosystem surfaces.
- **Exclusivity:** Category exclusivity (value retail) is the central prize and should be negotiated explicitly.

### 9.2 Model B — Campaign Partnership

- **What it is:** Performance-oriented campaigns (back-to-school, PEP Cell, parent household, teen conversion) run through the channel, individually or as a programme.
- **What PEP gets:** Attributable conversion — redemptions, store visits, activations — campaign by campaign, with full measurement.
- **Indicative commercial structure (proposed / illustrative):** Per-campaign fee and/or a performance component (e.g., tied to redemptions or activations), allowing PEP to pay in proportion to results.
- **Rights:** Campaign-level segmentation, creative placement, and reporting access.
- **Exclusivity:** Campaign-window category exclusivity (e.g., sole value-retail back-to-school partner for a given cycle).

### 9.3 Model C — Digital Services Growth Partner

- **What it is:** The deepest model — PEP / Pepkor as the strategic digital-services growth partner of the ecosystem, integrating PEP Cell, Flash, bill payments, PAXI and the future bank into the channel's relevant flows (e.g., PayBridge fee payments routed through Pepkor rails; connectivity tied to school need; pre-bank warm-audience building).
- **What PEP gets:** Cross-BU growth, the pre-launch bank advantage, and the fullest version of the compounding relationship described in Section 6.
- **Indicative commercial structure (proposed / illustrative):** A strategic partnership combining a base fee with revenue-share or transaction-linked economics across services (payments, connectivity, fulfilment), reflecting that PEP captures value across multiple BUs.
- **Rights:** Deep integration rights, preferred-partner status for fintech/connectivity flows, and bank-readiness collaboration.
- **Exclusivity:** Broad category exclusivity across value retail and, by negotiation, preferred status in connectivity and fee-payment flows.

### 9.4 Investment vs. return framing

| Model | Indicative investment posture (illustrative) | Primary return | Strategic role |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A — Headline Sponsorship** | Fixed annual fee, tiered by reach | Category ownership + defensive moat | Claim the ground |
| **B — Campaign Partnership** | Variable, performance-linked | Attributable retail/connectivity conversion | Prove the conversion |
| **C — Digital Services Growth Partner** | Base + revenue/transaction share | Cross-BU growth + pre-bank advantage | Compound the relationship |

> **How to read this:** The recommended path is to *enter at the campaign level via the pilot* (lowest commitment, fastest proof), with a contractual option to step up to Headline Sponsorship and then Growth Partner as results land. PEP pays for proof first and ownership second — but secures the *option* on ownership now, before a competitor removes it. **All figures and structures are illustrative starting points, not quotes.**

## 10. Measurement Framework

A channel that cannot be measured cannot be defended in a budget meeting. The school channel's decisive advantage over interruptive media is that it is *natively measurable*. The framework below is **a proposed measurement framework**, to be finalised with PEP, and is designed so that every rand of spend ties to an observable outcome.

### 10.1 Proposed KPI set

| KPI category | Specific metrics | What it proves |
|---|---|---|
| **Reach** | Schools connected; parent/learner/teacher reach; households connected | Scale of the audience actually reached |
| **Engagement** | Impressions; opens; click-through rate (CTR); time-in-context | Whether the channel earns attention (vs. interruptive baselines) |
| **Conversion** | Voucher redemptions; store-locator clicks; in-store transactions; SIM activations; device units; data top-ups | Whether attention turns into revenue |
| **Funnel** | Notification → voucher → store-locator → redemption conversion rates at each step | Where value is created or lost; optimisation targets |
| **District/provincial performance** | Performance by province / district / school | Where the channel works hardest; where to scale next |
| **Bank-readiness (pre-launch)** | Warm-audience size; (consented) interest registration | Pre-April-2027 readiness for the bank |
| **Efficiency** | Cost per redemption / activation / acquisition vs. paid-media benchmarks | The economic case vs. alternative channels |

### 10.2 Proposed dashboard

We propose a single PEP-facing dashboard (built on the ProvinceBridge command-centre architecture that already exists) showing, in near-real-time and filterable by province/district/school/grade/role:

- Reach and engagement (impressions, CTR, opens)
- Conversion (redemptions, store-locator clicks, activations, transactions)
- Funnel conversion at each step
- District/provincial heat-mapping of performance
- Efficiency vs. paid-media benchmark
- Pre-bank warm-audience tracking

### 10.3 Attribution approach

To make conversion *credible*, we propose:

- **Control groups** — reached vs. matched-unreached households, to isolate incremental lift.
- **Unique, channel-specific voucher codes** — so redemptions are unambiguously attributable to the school channel.
- **First-party tie-back** — redemptions and activations reconciled against PEP's own POS/activation data.

> **Proposed framework, candidly labelled:** This is a proposed measurement framework, not a finalised contract spec. Its purpose is to commit, up front, that the partnership will be judged on attributable outcomes — redemptions, activations, store visits, acquisition cost — rather than on impressions alone. That commitment is itself a differentiator from conventional sponsorship.

## 11. Pilot Proposal

The pilot is designed to answer one question with real data: **does the school channel convert for PEP?** Everything about it is sized to be low-risk, fast, and decisive.

### 11.1 Pilot design

| Parameter | Proposal |
|---|---|
| **Scope** | 50–100 schools |
| **Duration** | 90 days |
| **Focus (choose one)** | (a) Back-to-school retail campaign, **or** (b) PEP Cell connectivity campaign |
| **Audience** | Parents and learners in the selected schools, segmented by grade/role |
| **Mechanics** | In-channel offers + unique digital vouchers + store-locator routing (+ PAXI where relevant) |
| **Tracking** | Engagement + retail-conversion tracking from day one, with control groups and unique codes |
| **Reporting** | Live dashboard (Section 10) for PEP throughout |

### 11.2 Why these parameters

- **50–100 schools** is large enough to produce statistically meaningful conversion data and to test segmentation, yet small enough to run cleanly and contain cost.
- **90 days** captures a full campaign arc — launch, sustain, convert, measure — and, if timed to the December–January cycle, captures the highest-intent retail moment and warms households immediately before the April 2027 bank launch.
- **Single focus** keeps attribution clean: one objective, one set of metrics, one clear verdict.

### 11.3 Success criteria (to be agreed with PEP up front)

Proposed criteria (illustrative thresholds to be set jointly with PEP):

- **Engagement:** CTR meaningfully above PEP's paid-media benchmark for the same offer.
- **Conversion:** Voucher redemption / activation rate above an agreed threshold, with positive incremental lift vs. control.
- **Efficiency:** Cost per redemption / activation at or below paid-media benchmark.
- **Qualitative:** Positive school and parent reception; no brand-safety or POPIA incidents.

### 11.4 Renewal decision gate

At **day 90**, a single decision gate: did the pilot meet the agreed success criteria?

- **If yes →** proceed to district expansion (Roadmap Phase 2) under a Campaign Partnership or Headline Sponsorship, timed to scale before April 2027.
- **If no →** PEP has spent a bounded, defined amount and gained real first-party insight into its school-household base, with no further commitment.

> **The pilot's whole purpose:** convert a strategic argument into a number PEP trusts, at a cost PEP can ignore if it fails. That asymmetry — small bounded cost, large strategic upside, clean decision gate — is the entire case for starting now.

## 12. Risks & Mitigations

We state the risks candidly. A partnership of this kind has real risks, and naming them precisely — with specific mitigations — is part of making it credible.

| # | Risk | Why it matters | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Brand / reputational** — PEP appearing to commercialise children or schools | Schools and parents are protective; mishandling damages trust on both sides | Strict, age-appropriate placement rules; no targeting of children with inappropriate offers; school-approved messaging; value-and-utility framing (vouchers, affordability) over pure advertising; school remains in control of its communication |
| 2 | **Data protection / POPIA** | Any household/learner data carries legal and reputational exposure under POPIA | Consent-based, governed data handling by design; data minimisation; segmentation at aggregate/role level rather than exposing individuals to PEP; clear roles (the platform governs data; PEP receives campaign performance, not raw personal data); POPIA-compliant processing agreements |
| 3 | **Execution** — scaling from pilot to province cleanly | A poor rollout undermines the channel's core promise of reliability | Start with the bounded pilot; lean on the already-live ProvinceBridge command-centre architecture and existing PEP-branded environments; phased roadmap with gates (Section 13) |
| 4 | **Measurement / attribution** — can conversion be proven? | Without credible attribution, the channel is just another sponsorship | Control groups, unique voucher codes, first-party POS tie-back (Section 10); agree success criteria *before* the pilot |
| 5 | **Partner perception** — "is this charity / CSI?" | Mis-framing reduces it to a hand-out and forfeits the commercial logic | Position relentlessly as a *commercial growth and channel-ownership* partnership; lead with conversion metrics and cross-BU growth; CSI value, if any, is a by-product, not the pitch |
| 6 | **Public-sector sensitivity** — perceived displacement of government systems | Education is politically sensitive; appearing to replace GDE/departmental systems is a risk | Position explicitly as *complementary* to — never a replacement for — government/provincial systems; operate alongside official infrastructure; respect departmental authority |
| 7 | **Channel clutter** — too many sponsors dilute trust | If the channel becomes a billboard, its trust advantage evaporates | Category exclusivity and disciplined placement limits; protect the channel's utility-first character as the source of its value |

> **The candour point:** None of these risks is disqualifying, and each has a concrete mitigation built into how the partnership is structured. The two that deserve board-level attention are **#2 (POPIA / data protection)** and **#1 (brand-safety around children)** — both are addressed by a consent-based, school-controlled, data-minimised design in which PEP receives campaign *performance*, not raw personal data. Getting these two right is non-negotiable and entirely achievable.

### 12.1 Net risk posture

It is worth stating the overall risk shape plainly, because risk framing often decides these conversations more than the risks themselves. The pilot is *bounded, reversible and time-limited*: a defined number of schools, a defined 90-day window, a defined spend, and a clean exit at the day-90 gate if criteria are not met. The two highest-stakes risks (data and child brand-safety) are *design-time* risks — they are controlled by how the partnership is built, not by chance, and the mitigations are well-understood and standard for any responsible consumer-data programme. The remaining risks (execution, attribution, perception, public-sector sensitivity, clutter) are *management* risks — addressed by phasing, measurement discipline, positioning discipline, and exclusivity.

Against that bounded downside sits an unbounded and largely *strategic* upside: category ownership, cross-BU growth, and a pre-warmed audience for a bank launch that will otherwise acquire customers cold. The risk asymmetry runs strongly in favour of acting — provided the data and brand-safety design is done properly, which it can be.

> **Risk verdict:** A contained, reversible, well-mitigated downside versus a strategic, compounding, hard-to-reverse upside. That is the risk profile of a move worth making — and the structure of the pilot is specifically designed to keep it that way.

## 14. Recommended Next Steps

The analysis points to one action. We keep it crisp.

1. **Authorise the 90-day pilot** across 50–100 schools, anchored to a single focus (back-to-school **or** PEP Cell), ideally timed to the December 2026–January 2027 cycle.
2. **Agree success criteria up front** — the engagement, conversion and efficiency thresholds in Section 11 — so the day-90 verdict is unambiguous.
3. **Stand up the measurement dashboard** (Section 10) on the existing ProvinceBridge architecture, with control groups, unique voucher codes, and first-party tie-back, before the pilot starts.
4. **Secure a contractual option on category exclusivity** (Model A) at pilot signing — so PEP locks the *right* to own the channel now, before a competitor removes it, while only paying for proof first.
5. **Convene a short working session** between PEP (strategy, fintech/PEP Cell, marketing) and the SchoolBridge team to confirm pilot schools, focus, criteria and timeline.

> **The call to action:** Authorise the pilot this quarter. It is a bounded, low-risk, fully-measured test of a channel that is already built and live — timed against a clock (April 2027) that is already running. The cost of acting is contained and known. The cost of waiting is to risk handing a category-defining, bank-enabling channel to a competitor. Start small, prove it with real numbers, and scale into the bank launch.
